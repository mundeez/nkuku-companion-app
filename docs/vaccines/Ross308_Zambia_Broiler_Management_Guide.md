# Ross 308 Broiler Vaccination & Management Guide for Zambia

**Version:** 1.0.0
**Date:** 2026-06-30
**Target:** Commercial Ross 308 broiler production in Lusaka, Zambia and similar climatic zones in Southern Africa
**Evidence level:** Evidence-based with citations and confidence levels

---

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Ross 308 Overview

The Ross 308 is one of the world's most widely used broiler genotypes. According to Aviagen, it is selected for "a balanced range of parent stock and broiler characteristics" including growth rate, feed conversion efficiency, meat yield, robustness, and adaptability to tropical environments. Ross 308 broilers are available in two forms:

- **Ross 308:** Slow-feathering, sexable in the hatchery (female broilers + slow-feathering males).
- **Ross 308 FF:** Fast-feathering, all birds feather at the same rate.

For the purpose of this guide, management recommendations apply to both unless otherwise stated, with performance data taken from the **Ross 308/308 FF Broiler Performance Objectives 2022**.

### 1.2 Production Cycle in Zambia

A typical commercial broiler cycle in Lusaka runs from **day-old chick (DOC) placement to processing at 35–42 days**, depending on target market weight (usually 1.8–2.5 kg live weight). The Lusaka climate features:

- **Hot, wet season:** November–April (rainy season, high humidity).
- **Cool, dry season:** May–August (cold nights, low humidity).
- **Hot, dry season:** September–November (high temperatures, low humidity).

These seasonal variations directly affect housing temperature, ventilation, water intake, and disease pressure. The guide therefore includes season-specific adjustments.

### 1.3 Importance of Vaccination

Newcastle disease (ND), infectious bursal disease (IBD/Gumboro), and infectious bronchitis (IB) are among the most economically damaging diseases in Zambian broiler production. Vaccination is the cornerstone of disease control, but it must be supported by:

- Good biosecurity.
- Correct cold chain and vaccine handling.
- Accurate administration technique.
- Timing that accounts for maternal antibodies.
- Effective coccidiosis control.
- Sound nutrition and environmental management.

### 1.4 Zambia-Specific Disease Considerations

Research from the University of Zambia and regional institutions confirms that **Newcastle disease virus (NDV) genotype VII.2** circulates in Lusaka Province alongside vaccine-related genotype II strains. This genotype VII.2 has been detected in both vaccinated and unvaccinated flocks, which indicates that vaccine strain selection and administration quality are critical. The most common diseases influencing vaccination decisions in Zambia are:

- Newcastle disease (ND).
- Infectious bursal disease (Gumboro).
- Infectious bronchitis (IB).
- Coccidiosis.
- Marek's disease (controlled by hatchery vaccination).
- Salmonellosis / colibacillosis (management-dependent, not routinely vaccinated in broilers).
- Fowl pox (area-dependent, more common in backyard/scavenging systems).

## 2. Disease Profiles

### 2.1 Newcastle Disease (ND)

| Attribute | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Cause** | Avian paramyxovirus type 1 (APMV-1), virulent NDV strains. |
| **Symptoms** | Sudden death, green diarrhea, respiratory distress, nervous signs (tremors, torticollis), swollen head, reduced feed intake. |
| **Mortality** | Up to 90–100% in unvaccinated flocks; highly variable in vaccinated flocks depending on strain and challenge. |
| **Economic impact** | High mortality, condemned carcasses, growth depression, treatment costs, trade restrictions. |
| **Prevention** | Biosecurity + vaccination with live or inactivated ND vaccines. Genotype-matched vaccines (e.g., VII.2) may improve protection where genotype VII strains circulate. |
| **Vaccination age** | Day 1 (hatchery coarse spray) or day 10–21 (drinking water/eye drop), with booster if kept beyond 42 days. |
| **Vaccine types** | Live attenuated (e.g., Hitchner B1, LaSota, Clone 30, C2, VG/GA strains); inactivated oil-emulsion for breeders/layers. |
| **Booster requirements** | In high-challenge areas or for birds kept >42 days, a second live ND vaccination at 28–35 days is common. |

**Evidence:** Mubamba et al. (2016) showed ND follows a seasonal, cyclic pattern in Eastern Zambia with peaks in the hot dry season. Musako & Abolnik (2012) reported 73% seroprevalence in unvaccinated backyard flocks. A 2024 UNZA study detected NDV genotype VII.2 in vaccinated commercial and backyard flocks in Lusaka Province, highlighting vaccine strain and administration gaps.

### 2.2 Infectious Bronchitis (IB)

| Attribute | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Cause** | Infectious bronchitis virus (IBV), a coronavirus; multiple serotypes/variants exist. |
| **Symptoms** | Respiratory signs (snicking, rales, gasping), reduced weight gain, poor feed conversion, kidney damage (nephropathogenic strains), reduced uniformity. |
| **Mortality** | Usually low in broilers but can be high if secondary E. coli infection occurs. |
| **Economic impact** | Growth depression, poor FCR, secondary bacterial infections, processing downgrades. |
| **Prevention** | Vaccination in hatchery or at first week, plus biosecurity and control of secondary E. coli. |
| **Vaccination age** | Day 1 (hatchery spray) and/or day 10–14 (coarse spray or drinking water). |
| **Vaccine types** | Live attenuated Massachusetts-type (e.g., H120, Ma5) and variant strains (e.g., 4/91, QX-like) where locally relevant. |
| **Booster requirements** | Often given at 10–14 days; no routine boosters for short broiler cycles unless high challenge. |

**Evidence:** Aviagen and MSD Animal Health recommend IB vaccination at day 1 in the hatchery via coarse spray, followed by a day 10 booster in high-challenge areas. The choice of IB strain should be based on local field isolate identification where possible.

---

### 2.3 Infectious Bursal Disease (Gumboro, IBD)

| Attribute | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Cause** | Infectious bursal disease virus (IBDV), a very stable Birnavirus. |
| **Symptoms** | Depression, watery diarrhea, vent picking, bursal swelling then atrophy, immunosuppression, sudden mortality spikes. |
| **Mortality** | 5–20% in standard strains; up to 60%+ with very virulent (vvIBDV) strains. |
| **Economic impact** | Direct mortality + severe immunosuppression leading to failures of ND/IB vaccination and secondary infections. |
| **Prevention** | Hatchery immune-complex vaccines (single dose) OR on-farm live intermediate/intermediate-plus vaccines timed to maternal antibody decline. |
| **Vaccination age** | Day 1 (hatchery, immune complex) OR day 14–18 (on-farm, depending on MDA). |
| **Vaccine types** | Live attenuated (D78, 228E, Lukert), intermediate-plus (Winterfield 2512), immune-complex (CEVAC Transmune), inactivated for breeders. |
| **Booster requirements** | Immune-complex vaccines provide lifelong protection. On-farm live vaccines may require one dose if timed correctly; a second dose in high-challenge areas. |

**Evidence:** The Deventer formula is the standard method to time IBD vaccination based on maternal antibody decline. Live intermediate-plus vaccines should not be given before 10 days of age or in birds without MDA. Ceva's Transmune immune-complex vaccine is designed to overcome MDA interference by providing a single hatchery dose.

### 2.4 Marek's Disease

| Attribute | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Cause** | Gallid herpesvirus 2 (MDV), an oncogenic herpesvirus. |
| **Symptoms** | Paralysis (one wing, leg, neck), weight loss, lymphomatous tumors in organs, immunosuppression. |
| **Mortality** | High in unvaccinated flocks; up to 30–50%. |
| **Economic impact** | Mortality, culls, condemnations, immunosuppression. |
| **Prevention** | Hatchery vaccination is standard and highly effective. |
| **Vaccination age** | Day 1, in ovo (18 days of incubation) or subcutaneous injection at hatch. |
| **Vaccine types** | HVT (turkey herpesvirus), SB-1, Rispens, or vector combinations (e.g., HVT-ND). |
| **Booster requirements** | None. |

**Evidence:** All commercial broilers should receive Marek's vaccine at the hatchery. This is non-negotiable for commercial production.

---

### 2.5 Coccidiosis

| Attribute | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Cause** | Eimeria species (E. acervulina, E. maxima, E. tenella, E. mitis, E. mivati). |
| **Symptoms** | Diarrhea (may be bloody), depression, huddling, poor feed conversion, weight loss, mortality in severe cases. |
| **Mortality** | Variable; subclinical coccidiosis is often more economically damaging than clinical mortality. |
| **Economic impact** | Major cause of poor FCR, gut damage, secondary bacterial enteritis (especially necrotic enteritis), and growth loss. |
| **Prevention** | Anticoccidial drugs (ionophores or chemicals) in feed, live coccidiosis vaccines, or bio-shuttle programs. |
| **Vaccination age** | Day 1 (hatchery spray or gel droplets) or first week of life via feed/water for some vaccines. |
| **Vaccine types** | Live attenuated (e.g., Paracox, Advent) or non-attenuated (e.g., Coccivac, Immucox). |
| **Booster requirements** | None. Vaccines rely on controlled cycling to develop immunity. |

**Evidence:** FAO and Aviagen emphasize that coccidiosis control is central to broiler performance. Anticoccidial resistance is common, so rotation/shuttle programs are recommended. Vaccines can restore sensitivity to anticoccidials when used in rotation.

---

### 2.6 Infectious Laryngotracheitis (ILT)

| Attribute | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Cause** | Gallid herpesvirus 1 (GaHV-1). |
| **Symptoms** | Severe respiratory distress, gasping, bloody mucus, conjunctivitis, high mortality in acute outbreaks. |
| **Mortality** | 5–50% depending on virulence. |
| **Economic impact** | Mortality, growth loss, secondary infections. |
| **Prevention** | Vaccination only in endemic areas or following veterinary advice. Live vaccines can spread and revert. |
| **Vaccination age** | Usually 4–6 weeks; not commonly used in short-cycle broilers unless high risk. |
| **Vaccine types** | Live attenuated eye-drop or drinking-water vaccines. |
| **Booster requirements** | Area-specific; typically one dose. |

**Evidence:** ILT is not routinely included in standard broiler schedules unless endemic. Decision should be based on local veterinary diagnosis and history.

### 2.7 Fowl Pox

| Attribute | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Cause** | Avipoxvirus. |
| **Symptoms** | Scabs on comb, wattles, eyelids, skin; wet form causes respiratory/canker lesions. |
| **Mortality** | Low in dry form; higher in wet form or with secondary infections. |
| **Economic impact** | Reduced growth, carcass downgrades, blindness. |
| **Prevention** | Vaccination in areas with history; mosquito control. |
| **Vaccination age** | Not routine in fast-growing broilers unless high risk. |
| **Vaccine types** | Live wing-web stab vaccine. |
| **Booster requirements** | None. |

**Evidence:** Fowl pox is more relevant to backyard and free-range poultry than fully housed commercial broilers. Vaccination decisions should be area-specific.

### 2.8 Avian Influenza (AI)

| Attribute | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Cause** | Influenza A virus (Orthomyxoviridae); highly pathogenic avian influenza (HPAI) is a notifiable disease. |
| **Symptoms** | Sudden high mortality, edema, cyanosis, diarrhea, nervous signs, respiratory distress. |
| **Mortality** | Up to 100% in HPAI. |
| **Economic impact** | Catastrophic mortality, flock depopulation, trade bans, zoonotic risk. |
| **Prevention** | Strict biosecurity, control of wild birds/waterfowl, official surveillance and control; vaccination only under national policy. |
| **Vaccination age** | Not routinely used in Zambian broilers unless government-approved emergency vaccination program. |
| **Vaccine types** | Inactivated HPAI vaccines where authorized. |
| **Booster requirements** | Per national policy. |

**Evidence:** HPAI is a controlled, notifiable disease. Any suspicion must be reported to the State Veterinarian. Vaccination is not a standard commercial broiler practice in Zambia and is subject to WOAH/FAO and national policy.

### 2.9 Salmonellosis

| Attribute | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Cause** | Salmonella enterica subspecies (e.g., S. Enteritidis, S. Typhimurium, S. Gallinarum). |
| **Symptoms** | Diarrhea, pasty vent, poor growth, mortality in young chicks; adults may be carriers. |
| **Mortality** | Variable; higher in young chicks. |
| **Economic impact** | Mortality, food safety risk, public health concern, trade restrictions. |
| **Prevention** | Biosecurity, hatchery hygiene, feed quality, rodent control, competitive exclusion (probiotics), vaccination of breeders. |
| **Vaccination age** | Not routine in broilers; breeder vaccination more common. |
| **Vaccine types** | Live or inactivated Salmonella vaccines for breeders. |
| **Booster requirements** | Per manufacturer program. |

**Evidence:** A Zambian study isolated Salmonella species from broiler and layer chicks, highlighting hatchery and early-life hygiene as critical control points.

### 2.10 Mycoplasma (Chronic Respiratory Disease)

| Attribute | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Cause** | Mycoplasma gallisepticum (MG) and M. synoviae (MS). |
| **Symptoms** | Chronic respiratory signs, sinusitis, airsacculitis, synovitis, reduced growth. |
| **Mortality** | Usually low unless complicated by E. coli or virulent viruses. |
| **Economic impact** | Poor growth, high condemnation, secondary infections, persistent flock problems. |
| **Prevention** | Biosecurity, sourcing MG/MS-negative parent stock, vaccination of breeders/pullets. |
| **Vaccination age** | Not routine in broilers. |
| **Vaccine types** | Live or inactivated MG/MS vaccines for breeders. |
| **Booster requirements** | Per breeder program. |

**Evidence:** Mycoplasma control starts with the parent stock and hatchery. Broilers from MG-negative sources rarely need vaccination.

### 2.11 E. coli / Colibacillosis

| Attribute | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Cause** | Avian pathogenic Escherichia coli (APEC). |
| **Symptoms** | Septicemia, yolk sac infection, omphalitis, airsacculitis, pericarditis, swollen head syndrome. |
| **Mortality** | Variable; often secondary to viral respiratory disease or immunosuppression. |
| **Economic impact** | Mortality, condemnations, treatment costs, poor FCR. |
| **Prevention** | Biosecurity, water quality, hatchery hygiene, control of predisposing diseases (IB, ND, Gumboro, mycoplasma). |
| **Vaccination age** | Not routine in most broilers; autogenous vaccines used in some problem farms. |
| **Vaccine types** | Autogenous bacterins. |
| **Booster requirements** | Per farm-specific veterinary advice. |

**Evidence:** Colibacillosis is usually a secondary disease. Effective control depends on preventing primary respiratory and immunosuppressive diseases.

---

### 2.12 Necrotic Enteritis

| Attribute | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Cause** | Clostridium perfringens Type A and C (often Type A with NetB toxin). |
| **Symptoms** | Sudden death, depression, diarrhea, ruffled feathers, enteritis lesions; often subclinical with poor FCR. |
| **Mortality** | Up to 50% in acute outbreaks; subclinical form is more common and costly. |
| **Economic impact** | Major cause of poor FCR, growth loss, and condemnation in antibiotic-free systems. |
| **Prevention** | Coccidiosis control, gut health management, avoid high non-starch polysaccharide grains, use of probiotics/acidifiers/phytogenics, judicious antibiotics only when veterinary indicated. |
| **Vaccination age** | No commercial vaccine; prevention is management-based. |
| **Vaccine types** | N/A. |
| **Booster requirements** | N/A. |

**Evidence:** Necrotic enteritis is strongly linked to coccidiosis damage (especially E. acervulina and E. maxima). Merck Veterinary Manual identifies coccidiosis prevention as the main strategy for NE control. Antibiotic treatment is effective but should be reserved for clinical cases under veterinary supervision.

---

### 2.13 Other Diseases Relevant to Zambia

- **Heat stress / heat stroke:** Not an infectious disease but a major cause of mortality and poor growth in Lusaka's hot season. Covered under emergency response.
- **Ascites / sudden death syndrome:** Metabolic conditions linked to fast growth, high altitude, and poor ventilation. Lusaka is at approximately 1,280 m altitude, so ascites risk is moderate and ventilation is critical.
- **Leg problems / skeletal disorders:** Linked to rapid growth, poor brooding, nutrient imbalance, and slippery litter.

## 3. Vaccination Programme

### 3.1 Core Principles

1. **Hatchery vaccination is preferred when available:** Marek's, ND, IB, and immune-complex IBD vaccines are best administered at day-old or in ovo because they provide early, uniform protection and reduce field stress.
2. **On-farm vaccination must be timed to maternal antibodies:** Live vaccines given too early are neutralized by maternal antibodies; given too late, birds may be exposed to field challenge before immunity develops. Use the Deventer formula for IBD timing if not using an immune-complex product.
3. **Cold chain must never be broken:** Live vaccines must be stored and transported at 2–8°C. Reconstituted vaccine must be used within 2 hours (or per manufacturer label).
4. **Water quality and withdrawal are critical for drinking-water vaccines:** Use cool, non-chlorinated water. Add skim milk or vaccine stabilizer. Withhold water for 30–60 minutes before vaccination in hot weather; 1–2 hours in cool weather.
5. **Record everything:** Date, age, vaccine name, batch, expiry, dose, route, administrator, and flock reaction.

### 3.2 Recommended Vaccination Schedule for Lusaka, Zambia

The following schedule is designed for a **42-day commercial broiler cycle** in a region with **high ND and IBD challenge**. It integrates hatchery and on-farm options because not all farms have access to hatchery vaccination services. Two versions are provided:

- **Option A:** Hatchery vaccination available (preferred).
- **Option B:** On-farm vaccination only.

#### Option A — Hatchery Vaccination Available (Preferred)

| Day | Age | Disease | Vaccine Examples | Route | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Day-old | Marek's disease | HVT, SB-1, Rispens, or vector | In ovo or subcutaneous | Standard hatchery practice. |
| 1 | Day-old | Newcastle disease + IB | Nobilis ND C2 + IB Ma5, or Vectormune ND | Coarse spray or eye drop | Provides early respiratory protection. |
| 1 | Day-old | Gumboro (IBD) | CEVAC Transmune, or similar immune-complex | Subcutaneous or in ovo | Single dose; overcomes MDA interference. |
| 1 | Day-old | Coccidiosis | Fortegra, Advent, Coccivac, Paracox | Coarse spray or gel | Optional; use if coccidiosis history or bio-shuttle program. |
| 10 | 10 days | IB + ND (booster) | Nobilis IB MA5 + Clone 30 | Drinking water or coarse spray | Booster in high-challenge areas. |
| 21 | 21 days | Newcastle disease | Nobilis ND Clone 30 or LaSota | Drinking water | Booster if high ND challenge or birds kept >42 days. |
| 28 | 28 days | Newcastle disease | LaSota or Clone 30 | Drinking water | Only if birds are kept >42 days or severe challenge. |

#### Option B — On-Farm Vaccination Only

| Day | Age | Disease | Vaccine Examples | Route | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Day-old | Marek's disease | Must be administered at hatchery | — | **Cannot be done on-farm effectively.** Source chicks from hatchery-vaccinated parent flocks. |
| 5–7 | 5–7 days | Newcastle disease + IB | Hitchner B1 or Clone 30 + H120/Ma5 | Eye drop or coarse spray | Early respiratory protection if no hatchery vaccination. |
| 14–16 | 14–16 days | Gumboro (IBD) | Nobilis Gumboro D78 or intermediate-plus | Drinking water | **Time using Deventer formula or MDA assessment.** |
| 18–21 | 18–21 days | Newcastle disease | Clone 30 or LaSota | Drinking water | Critical in high-challenge areas. |
| 28–30 | 28–30 days | Gumboro (IBD) | D78 or 228E | Drinking water | Only in high-challenge areas or if first dose was very early. |
| 35 | 35 days | Newcastle disease | LaSota | Drinking water | Only if birds are kept >42 days. |

> **Important:** If hatchery vaccination is unavailable for Marek's, source chicks from a hatchery that provides Marek's vaccination. On-farm Marek's vaccination at day-old is technically possible but rarely achieves the uniformity and timing of hatchery administration.

### 3.3 Vaccination Administration Details

| Parameter | Guidance |
| --- | --- |
| **Water withdrawal** | 30–60 minutes in hot weather; 1–2 hours in cool weather. Water must be available immediately after. |
| **Water temperature** | 18–21°C. Do not use chlorinated water. Add skim milk powder (2 g/L) or commercial stabilizer. |
| **Vaccine mixing** | Reconstitute in cool, clean water. Use vaccine within 1–2 hours of reconstitution (follow label). |
| **Cold chain** | Maintain 2–8°C from purchase to administration. Use insulated cool boxes with ice packs. Do not freeze. |
| **Storage** | Refrigerator dedicated to vaccines; monitor temperature twice daily. |
| **Shelf life** | Use before expiry date. Do not use expired vaccines. |
| **Administration precautions** | Vaccinate healthy birds only. Avoid vaccination during stress, heat, or active disease. |
| **Common mistakes** | Breaking cold chain, chlorinated water, late vaccination, under-dosing, over-withholding water, vaccinating sick birds. |
| **Contraindications** | Do not use live vaccines in clinically ill flocks. Do not use intermediate-plus IBD vaccines in birds without MDA or under 10 days. |

### 3.4 Maternal Antibody Considerations

- **Maternal antibodies (MDA)** are passed from the hen to the chick via the yolk and provide early protection but interfere with live vaccines.
- **MDA half-life for IBD** is approximately **3 days** in broiler chicks.
- The **Deventer formula** calculates the optimal IBD vaccination day based on day-old MDA titers. Practical rule: if day-old titers are high, vaccinate around day 16–18; if low, vaccinate around day 10–14.
- **Immune-complex IBD vaccines** (e.g., CEVAC Transmune) are specifically designed to overcome MDA interference and allow a single day-old dose.
- For ND, MDA interference is less pronounced than for IBD, especially with coarse spray administration at day-old.

### 3.5 Hatchery vs On-Farm Vaccination

| Factor | Hatchery Vaccination | On-Farm Vaccination |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Timing** | Precise, uniform, early | Variable, dependent on farm schedule |
| **Stress** | Minimal; birds already in controlled environment | Higher; catching, handling, and house stress |
| **Uniformity** | High (spray cabinets, in ovo injection) | Lower; depends on operator skill and drinker coverage |
| **Cost** | Higher initial cost, lower labor cost | Lower vaccine cost, higher labor cost |
| **Diseases covered** | Marek's, ND, IB, IBD immune-complex, coccidiosis | ND, IB, IBD (live), coccidiosis (if applicable) |
| **Best for** | Large commercial operations, uniform flocks | Small farms without hatchery access |
| **Zambia context** | Preferred where hatchery services exist | Common for smallholders and some commercial farms |

## 4. Medication Guidance

### 4.1 Antimicrobial Stewardship

**Do not use antibiotics routinely for prophylaxis.** Antibiotic use should be:

- Based on a veterinary diagnosis or prescription.
- Used only when bacterial disease is confirmed or strongly suspected.
- Given at the correct dose and duration.
- Recorded with batch number, withdrawal period, and treatment dates.
- Withdrawn for the full period before slaughter.

### 4.2 When Antibiotics May Be Appropriate

- **Colibacillosis / E. coli septicemia:** confirmed by post-mortem or bacterial culture.
- **Necrotic enteritis:** acute clinical cases with confirmed C. perfringens involvement; use water medication.
- **Secondary bacterial infections after viral respiratory disease:** under veterinary supervision.
- **Chronic respiratory disease (Mycoplasma complicated):** usually in breeders, not broilers.

### 4.3 Withdrawal Periods

| Class / Product | Typical Withdrawal (days) | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Tetracyclines | 7–14 | Check product label. |
| Fluoroquinolones | 7–14 | Restricted/critical use; veterinary prescription only. |
| Sulfonamides | 10–15 | Check product label. |
| Macrolides (e.g., tylosin) | 5–7 | Check product label. |
| Aminoglycosides | 7–14 | Check product label. |
| Ionophores (coccidiostats) | 0–5 | Usually 0–3 days withdrawal before slaughter. |

> **Always follow the product label and veterinary advice.** Withdrawal periods vary by country registration and formulation.

### 4.4 Non-Antibiotic Supportive Care

| Product | Purpose | Evidence Level | Status |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Probiotics / competitive exclusion** | Gut health, reduce pathogen colonization | Moderate | Recommended |
| **Acidifiers (organic acids)** | Water sanitation, gut pH modulation | Moderate | Recommended |
| **Electrolytes** | Rehydration after stress, heat, transport, vaccination | Strong | Recommended during stress/heat |
| **Vitamins (A, D, E, B-complex)** | Support immunity, growth, stress recovery | Strong | Recommended during stress |
| **Stress packs (vitamin + electrolyte)** | Post-vaccination, heat stress, transport | Strong | Recommended during stress |
| **Liver/kidney tonics** | Support metabolic function after disease or medication | Limited | Optional; veterinary advice |
| **Coccidiostats (ionophores / chemicals)** | Coccidiosis prevention | Strong | Required or recommended in feed |
| **Dewormers** | Internal parasites | Limited relevance in broilers | Generally not required in short-cycle broilers |
| **Phytogenics (essential oils, herbs)** | Gut health, antimicrobial alternatives | Moderate | Optional |

### 4.5 Coccidiostats

Coccidiostats are a standard component of broiler feed in Zambia and are **not classified as antibiotics** in most regulatory contexts. They are categorized as:

- **Ionophores:** monensin, salinomycin, narasin, lasalocid, maduramycin, semduramicin.
- **Chemical (synthetic) coccidiostats:** nicarbazine, diclazuril, robenidine, halofuginone, decoquinate.

**Best practice:** Use a rotation or shuttle program to slow resistance. Avoid using the same product class continuously for more than 2–3 grow-outs. Include a withdrawal period before slaughter as per label.

## 5. Broiler Management Guide

### 5.1 Brooding (Days 0–14)

#### Environmental Targets at Placement

| Parameter | Whole-House Brooding | Spot Brooding |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Air temperature at chick height | 30°C (86°F) | 32°C (89.6°F) at brooder edge |
| Floor temperature | 28–30°C (82.4–86°F) | 28–32°C (82.4–89.6°F) |
| Relative humidity | 60–70% for first 3 days; 50% thereafter | 60–70% for first 3 days; 50% thereafter |
| Light intensity | 30–40 lux (2.8–3.7 fc) | 80–100 lux (7.4–9.3 fc) in brooding area |
| Ventilation | Minimum ventilation from day 1; no drafts | Minimum ventilation from day 1; no drafts |

| Bird Age | Target Air Temperature (°C) | Target Air Temperature (°F) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Day-old | 30 | 86 |
| Day 3 | 28 | 82 |
| Day 6 | 27 | 81 |

### 5.2 Nutrition

#### Feeding Phases (Ross 308)

| Phase | Age | Target Crude Protein | Target Energy (ME kcal/kg) | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Pre-starter | 0–3 days | 23–24% | 3,000–3,025 | Highly digestible; encourage intake. |
| Starter | 0–10 days | 22–23% | 2,975–3,000 | Pellet/crumble; easy access. |
| Grower | 11–24 days | 20–21% | 3,050–3,075 | Transition to grower. |
| Finisher 1 | 25–39 days | 19–20% | 3,100–3,125 | Adjust to market weight. |
| Finisher 2 | 40–market | 18–19% | 3,125–3,150 | Withdrawal feed if required. |

> **Note:** These are typical targets based on Aviagen 2022 Nutrition Specifications. Actual feed formulations should be prepared by a qualified nutritionist and depend on ingredient availability and cost in Zambia (soybean meal, maize, fishmeal, sunflower cake, etc.).

#### Feed Physical Form

- **Crumbs/pellets:** 1.5–2.5 mm for starter; 2.5–3.5 mm for grower/finisher.
- **Avoid fines:** Fines reduce feed intake and growth.
- **Starter on paper:** Place 40 g/chick on paper trays for first 3 days to stimulate intake.

### 5.3 Water Management

- **Water temperature:** 18–21°C ideal. Below 5°C or above 30°C reduces intake.
- **Water-to-feed ratio:**
  - Bell drinkers: ~1.8:1 at 21°C.
  - Nipple drinkers: ~1.6–1.7:1 at 21°C.
- **Hot weather:** Water intake increases by approximately **6–7% per °C above 21°C**. Flush lines twice daily to keep water cool.
- **Water quality:** Test regularly for bacterial load, pH, minerals, and nitrates. Chlorinate source water if needed, but ensure it is non-chlorinated at vaccination time.
- **Drinker space:** 10–12 birds per nipple; 8 bell drinkers per 1,000 chicks in brooding area.

### 5.4 Litter Management

- Use **wood shavings, rice hulls, or chopped straw** as litter.
- Litter depth: 5–10 cm.
- Litter should be **dry and friable**; wet litter causes coccidiosis, footpad dermatitis, and ammonia buildup.
- Turn or top-dress litter as needed during the grow-out.
- Preheat house and litter to 28–30°C before chick arrival.

### 5.5 Stocking Density

| System | Recommended Density | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Closed, environmentally controlled house | 30–38 kg/m² | Higher densities require excellent ventilation. |
| Open-sided / naturally ventilated house | 20–30 kg/m² | Lusaka hot season requires lower density. |
| Hot season | Reduce by 10–15% | Reduce heat load and improve bird comfort. |

### 5.6 Biosecurity

| Practice | Implementation |
| --- | --- |
| **Farm perimeter fencing** | Prevent unauthorized entry; single access point. |
| **Footbaths** | At every house entrance; refresh disinfectant daily. |
| **Protective clothing** | Farm-specific boots and overalls for staff and visitors. |
| **Visitor log** | Record all visitors, date, purpose, and previous farm contact. |
| **All-in / all-out** | Empty, clean, disinfect, and rest between flocks (downtime 7–14 days). |
| **Rodent control** | Continuous baiting and trapping; rodents spread Salmonella and coccidiosis. |
| **Wild bird control** | Netting, sealed feed stores; wild birds can carry ND, AI, and Salmonella. |
| **Feed storage** | Dry, secure, pest-free; first-in-first-out. |
| **Dead bird disposal** | Daily removal; compost, incinerate, or deep bury. Do not leave in house. |
| **Cleaning and disinfection** | Dry cleaning → detergent wash → disinfectant → fumigation if appropriate. |

### 5.7 Farm Records

Maintain daily records of:

- Temperature and humidity (minimum twice daily).
- Mortality and culls (number, age, cause if known).
- Feed and water consumption.
- Vaccination and medication history.
- Body weight and uniformity (weekly).
- Litter quality score.
- Visitor log and biosecurity incidents.
- Weather conditions.

## 6. Emergency Response

### 6.1 Heat Stress Management

Lusaka experiences temperatures above 30°C in the hot season. Heat stress can cause mortality, poor growth, and poor meat quality.

| Sign | Action |
| --- | --- |
| Panting, wings spread, reduced feeding | Increase ventilation; check drinkers; reduce stocking density. |
| Temperature + humidity index high | Use evaporative cooling (if RH <75%); foggers with fans. |
| Water temperature >30°C | Flush lines; insulate tanks; shade water lines. |
| Daytime peaks | Feed during cooler hours; provide extra water access. |
| Power failure | Maintain backup generator; open curtains temporarily; use manual foggers. |

**General heat stress measures:**

- Reduce stocking density by 10–15% in hot season.
- Ensure roof insulation; spray external roof cautiously.
- Use circulation fans to create air movement over birds.
- Provide vitamins/electrolytes in water during heat stress.
- Ensure water is cooler than air temperature.

### 6.2 Cold Stress Management

Lusaka nights can drop below 10°C in the cool season (May–August). Cold stress increases mortality, poor growth, and feed wastage.

| Sign | Action |
| --- | --- |
| Chicks huddling, noisy, reduced activity | Increase brooding temperature; check heaters; reduce drafts. |
| Litter too cold | Preheat house longer before placement; add bedding. |
| Low humidity | Use humidifiers or open water surfaces cautiously. |
| Feed intake high but growth poor | Check house temperature and ventilation. |

### 6.3 Power Failures

- Install and maintain a **backup generator** with automatic transfer switch.
- Test generator weekly.
- Maintain minimum ventilation and water supply during outages.
- Have manual ventilation options (open curtains, fans on battery/inverter).
- In hot weather, power failure is an emergency; prioritize ventilation and water.

### 6.4 Disease Outbreak Response

| Step | Action |
| --- | --- |
| 1. Isolate | Quarantine affected house; restrict movement. |
| 2. Diagnose | Call a veterinarian; collect samples for laboratory diagnosis (post-mortem, PCR, culture). |
| 3. Treat | Only under veterinary guidance; use correct antibiotics/antimicrobials if bacterial. |
| 4. Biosecurity | Enhance disinfection, footbaths, visitor control. |
| 5. Record | Document mortality pattern, clinical signs, treatment, and outcomes. |
| 6. Report | For notifiable diseases (ND, HPAI), report to State Veterinarian immediately. |
| 7. Review | After outbreak, review vaccination, biosecurity, and management; adjust program. |

## 8. Zambia-Specific Considerations and Recommendations

### 8.1 Newcastle Disease Genotype VII.2

The detection of **NDV genotype VII.2** in Lusaka Province in both vaccinated and unvaccinated flocks means that:

- Vaccine strain selection matters. Genotype-matched or recombinant vaccines may provide better protection than genotype II vaccines in high-challenge areas.
- Vaccination quality (cold chain, dose, timing, route) is as important as vaccine choice.
- Biosecurity remains essential because vaccines reduce but do not eliminate field virus circulation.

### 8.2 Seasonal Adjustments

| Season | Management Focus |
| --- | --- |
| **Hot, wet (Nov–Apr)** | Heat stress, humidity, water quality, increased coccidiosis pressure, mosquitoes (fowl pox risk). |
| **Cool, dry (May–Aug)** | Brooding temperature, cold drafts, low humidity, water line freezing risk (rare). |
| **Hot, dry (Sep–Nov)** | Peak heat stress, dust, respiratory challenge, water demand. |

### 8.3 Vaccine Availability in Zambia

Vaccines commonly used in Zambia include:

- **Newcastle disease:** Lasota, Clone 30, C2, Vectormune ND (where available).
- **Gumboro/IBD:** D78, 228E, Transmune (immune-complex), local intermediate-plus products.
- **Infectious bronchitis:** H120, Ma5.
- **Marek's:** HVT-based products from hatcheries.
- **Coccidiosis:** Live vaccines (e.g., Fortegra, Advent) and in-feed anticoccidials.

Vaccine supply can be inconsistent. Farms should:

- Source from reputable suppliers.
- Verify cold chain during transport.
- Maintain a minimum stock of critical vaccines.
- Coordinate with veterinary suppliers in Lusaka and other major centers.

### 8.4 Recommended Schedule for Lusaka (Summary)

For a **42-day commercial Ross 308 broiler cycle** in Lusaka, the recommended vaccination and management program is:

1. **Hatchery:** Marek's, ND + IB coarse spray, IBD immune-complex (if available), optional coccidiosis vaccine.
2. **Day 10:** ND + IB booster (drinking water or spray) in high-challenge areas.
3. **Day 14–18:** On-farm IBD live vaccine only if hatchery immune-complex not used; time using MDA assessment.
4. **Day 21:** ND booster (drinking water).
5. **Day 28:** ND booster only if birds kept >42 days.
6. **Coccidiosis control:** Anticoccidial feed program or live vaccine; maintain rotation/shuttle strategy.
7. **Heat stress management:** Essential in Lusaka's hot season; reduce stocking density, provide cool water, maximize ventilation.
8. **Biosecurity:** Strict all-in/all-out, footbaths, rodent control, visitor control.
9. **Records:** Daily mortality, feed/water, temperature, humidity, weights, vaccinations, medications.
